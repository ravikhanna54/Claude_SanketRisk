Introduction & Inspection Setup INTRO

The Introduction section establishes the administrative identity of the inspection. Accuracy here is critical — these fields drive report headers, database records, and assignment tracking.

Inspection ID

Auto-generated on form load (format: INS-YYYY-MMDD-###). Do not alter unless directed by your QA reviewer. This is the primary record key used across all platform functions.

Purpose of Inspection

Select the correct purpose before beginning data entry. The purpose drives the underwriting context for the entire report. Common purposes: New Business (first-time inspection for new policy), Renewal (periodic re-inspection), Mid-Term (triggered by change in occupancy or risk), Re-Inspection (follow-up on prior recommendations).

Inspection Date

Always enter the actual date the site visit occurred, not the report completion date. These may differ by several days. The inspection date governs the currency of the risk assessment.

Tip: Complete the Intro section before arriving on site. Pre-populate from the inspection request if one exists — use the Convert button in the dashboard to auto-fill insured details.

Accurately identifying the insured entity and property location is foundational. Errors here affect policy matching, geocoding, and catastrophe modelling.

Construction CONSTRUCTION

Construction is one of the four COPE elements (Construction, Occupancy, Protection, Exposure) that form the backbone of commercial property underwriting. Accuracy is essential for rate adequacy and replacement cost estimation.

ISO Construction Class

Class 1 — Frame: Exterior walls and structural members are wood or other combustible material. Highest fire vulnerability.

Class 2 — Joisted Masonry: Exterior walls of masonry (brick, concrete block, stone) with wood floor and roof systems. Very common in older commercial buildings.

Class 3 — Non-Combustible: Exterior walls, floors and roof of metal or other non-combustible material, but not fire-resistive rated. Steel construction with metal deck.

Class 4 — Masonry Non-Combustible: Masonry exterior walls with non-combustible (metal) floors and roof. Better than Class 3 due to masonry walls.

Class 5 — Modified Fire Resistive: Construction with fire-resistive rated assemblies but not meeting full Class 6 requirements. Includes some concrete tilt-up.

Class 6 — Fire Resistive: All structural elements have minimum 2-hour fire resistance ratings. Concrete frame, fire-rated steel. Lowest fire vulnerability.

Year Built & Renovation

Always ask about the original construction year AND any major renovations. A 1960s building with a 2015 full renovation may have substantially improved electrical and mechanical systems. Document both dates and the scope of renovation.

Buildings constructed before 1970 warrant additional scrutiny for: knob-and-tube wiring, asbestos-containing materials, inadequate electrical service, and non-code-compliant structural elements.

Roof Assessment

Age: Most insurers apply surcharges or restrictions on roofs over 20 years old. Record the year the current roof covering was installed (not year built).

Type: Flat/low slope roofs require membrane inspection. Note any ponding water, blistering, or seam separation. Sloped roofs — assess shingle condition, ridge caps, and flashing.

Drains: Flat roofs must have functional primary and secondary (overflow) drains. Clogged drains can cause structural collapse under water load.

Mixed Construction: Use the percentage breakdown fields for buildings with more than one construction type. The ISO class assigned should reflect the predominant construction of the insured building(s).
Occupancy OCCUPANCY

Occupancy is the second COPE element. It describes what the building is used for — which directly determines fire load, hazard class, applicable supplements, and underwriting appetite.

Description of Operations

Write a clear, factual narrative of what occurs in the building. Include: primary products or services, production processes, number of employees, shifts operated, customer access, and any storage of raw materials or finished goods. A well-written description allows the underwriter to visualize the risk without visiting the site.

Owner-Occupied vs. Tenant

Owner-Occupied: The property owner operates from the building. Generally more stable — owner has direct incentive to maintain the property.

Tenant: A third party occupies the building. Confirm: Is there a lease? Who is responsible for fire protection maintenance? Are building alterations permitted?

Mixed: Some floors/units owner-occupied, others leased. Document the breakdown and identify tenant occupancies separately.

Hazardous Operations & Materials

Select all applicable hazardous processes. Each selection opens a detailed supplementary panel — complete all fields within each active panel. The NFPA Hazard Category should reflect the overall hazard classification of the occupancy, taking into account all processes present.

When multiple hazards coexist (e.g., welding AND flammable liquid storage), the combined risk is greater than any single hazard alone. Elevate the NFPA category accordingly and document the interaction in the Hazard Notes field.

Supplement Triggers: Certain occupancies require completion of a Supplement module. Activate the appropriate supplement via the Supplement Manager: Restaurant (cooking operations), Woodworking (millwork/cabinet shops), Warehouse (storage operations), Auto Service (garages/body shops), Sprinkler (sprinklered buildings), Apartments (multi-residential).
Protection PROTECTION

Protection is the third COPE element. It covers both private fire protection (sprinklers, alarms, extinguishers) and public protection (fire department response). Together these are the most significant rating variables after construction class.

Sprinkler Systems

Coverage: Note whether sprinklers cover the entire building or are partial. Partial coverage is a significant deficiency — document exactly which areas are unprotected and why.

Type: Wet pipe (most common — water filled at all times), Dry pipe (air/nitrogen pressurized — used in unheated spaces), Pre-action (requires dual activation — used in data centres, museums), Deluge (all heads open simultaneously — for high-hazard areas).

Condition: Check for: corrosion on heads or piping, painted heads (renders them ineffective — grounds for immediate recommendation), obstructions within 18 inches of heads, and evidence of recent service tag.

If the building has a sprinkler system, activate the Sprinkler Supplement for full system documentation.

Fire Alarm Systems

Local alarm: Sounds at the building only. No automatic notification of fire department — relies on occupants to call 911. Lowest level of protection.

Central station monitored: Connects to a ULC-listed monitoring centre that notifies fire department 24/7. Preferred by most underwriters.

Smoke vs. heat detection: Smoke detection responds earlier in fire development. Heat detectors are used in dusty or steamy environments where smoke detectors would false-alarm. Document which type is installed and coverage areas.

FUS / Public Protection

The Fire Underwriters Survey (FUS) grades assess the adequacy of municipal fire protection. Key factors: distance to responding fire hall, department type (full-time paid vs. volunteer), equipment, water supply, and communications.

In Canada, FUS grades run from 1 (best) to 10 (no protection). Grades 1–4 are preferred by most insurers. Grades 8–10 attract significant surcharges or restrictions.

For rural properties, note: nearest fire hall distance, tanker shuttle capability, and available water supply (cistern, pond, municipal hydrant).

Extinguisher Tip: Verify that portable extinguishers are: correct type for the hazard (ABC dry chemical is standard; CO2 for electrical/server rooms; Class K for commercial cooking), within service date (annual inspection tag), accessible and unobstructed, and that staff have received training on use.
Exposure EXPOSURE

Exposure is the fourth COPE element. It addresses risks originating outside the insured building — neighbouring properties, natural hazards, and environmental factors.

Neighbouring Exposures

Assess each compass direction. For each neighbouring property, record: occupancy type, construction class (if determinable from exterior), distance from the insured building, and any mitigating factors (firewalls, setbacks, separation). A vacant lot or parking area provides excellent exposure protection; a woodworking shop or auto body operation immediately adjacent is a serious exposure.

Minimum separation distances for satisfactory exposure: Frame to Frame — 50 ft. Frame to Masonry — 30 ft. Masonry to Masonry — 20 ft. These are general thresholds — exact requirements vary by insurer appetite.

Natural Hazard Exposure

Flood: Confirm FEMA flood zone (US) or floodplain mapping (Canada). Basement vs. slab construction matters significantly. Document any flood mitigation measures: sump pumps, backflow preventers, site grading.

Earthquake: Note the seismic zone and any seismic upgrades. Unreinforced masonry (URM) buildings in seismic zones are a particular concern — flag for underwriter.

Wind/Hail: Note roof age and condition, roof-to-wall attachment, and any storm shutters or impact-resistant glazing for high-wind areas.

Natural Catastrophe NAT-CAT

This section combines reference climatic and seismic data from the National Building Code of Canada (NBC) 2025, Appendix C, with your on-site observations of earthquake, wind/storm, and flood/water exposure. Together these produce a quantitative Nat-Cat grade for the property — a third independent SRC grading alongside the Property (COPE) and Sprinkler grades.

NBC 2025 Reference Location Lookup

Select the NBC reference location nearest the inspected property. This auto-populates the climatic design data table — ground snow load, wind pressures (1-in-10, 1-in-50, and 1-in-500-year), rainfall, annual precipitation, and driving rain wind pressure — along with the property's seismic hazard tier (Low, Moderate, High, or Very High), derived from the location's Smax value.

Choose the location based on geography, not administrative boundaries — a property on the coast should use a coastal reference point even if its mailing address lists an inland city. The reference location and elevation also appear in the Formal and Narrative reports, with the NRC source citation.

Required for Grading: The seismic hazard tier from this lookup is used to weight the earthquake demerits in the grade calculator. A reference location must be selected before the Nat-Cat grade can be calculated — if you tap Calculate Grade without one, the calculator will prompt you to select a location first.
Earthquake — Site & Building Observations
FieldGuidance
Construction Era vs. Seismic CodeCompare the building's construction or major retrofit date to when modern seismic provisions were adopted in the applicable code. Buildings built or substantially retrofitted to post-2005 standards generally carry the lowest demerit; pre-1970 construction with no seismic upgrade carries the highest.
Structural VulnerabilityAssess the structural system itself — unreinforced masonry (URM) bearing walls, soft-story conditions, or unbraced parapets indicate elevated vulnerability regardless of age. Conventional, well-maintained construction with no obvious weaknesses rates as low or moderate.
Equipment & Racking AnchorageNote whether storage racking, rooftop units, tanks, and heavy equipment are anchored or braced. Unanchored racking and equipment are a common source of post-earthquake loss even when the building structure performs well.
Sprinkler System Seismic BracingCheck for seismic bracing on sprinkler mains and branch lines per NFPA 13 — flexible couplings at floor/wall penetrations, sway bracing, and clearance around piping. Where not applicable (no sprinklers), select the not-applicable option.

FieldGuidance
Site Elevation vs. SurroundingsIs the building pad elevated above, level with, or below the surrounding grade? Elevated sites shed water away from the building and rate best.

Nat-Cat Grade Calculator

The calculator displays separate demerit subtotals for Earthquake, Wind/Storm, and Flood/Water, the seismic tier weighting applied to the earthquake subtotal, an overall score out of 100, and the resulting grade (1–5).

The Final Assigned Grade auto-fills from the calculation but can be changed by the inspector — if you do so, enter a brief reason in the Override field (e.g. a seismic retrofit completed after the construction-era selection was made). Site Location Map

Use the Site Location Map card (in the Exposure section) to confirm the address resolves to the correct property — select Satellite or Road Map view and tap Load Map. The Formal and Narrative reports include printable aerial and area maps automatically once a key is configured.

Loss History LOSS

Loss history is a critical underwriting data point. Your role is to accurately record what is presented and to note any inconsistencies or concerns.

Recording Losses

Record all losses for the standard 5-year period. For each loss: date, cause (peril), gross incurred amount, and current status (open/closed). If the insured claims no losses, note this explicitly — do not leave the section blank.

Pay attention to: frequency (multiple small losses may indicate poor management or fraud), severity relative to building value, and whether recommended repairs from prior inspections were completed.

Inconsistency Flag: If the site shows evidence of recent fire damage, water damage, or significant repairs that are not reflected in the stated loss history, document your observations in the Loss Comments field and flag for underwriter review. Do not accuse — describe what you observed objectively.
Safety & Compliance SAFETY

The safety section assesses life safety systems and general compliance with fire and building codes. Deficiencies here may constitute minimum standards violations and must be documented regardless of their impact on rating.

Exit & Egress

Verify: all required exits are marked with illuminated exit signs, exit doors open in the direction of travel (outward), no exits are blocked or locked during occupancy, and emergency lighting is present and functional (test by covering the sensor or pressing the test button).

Housekeeping

Poor housekeeping is one of the most common and significant fire risk factors. Assess: combustible waste accumulation, storage in mechanical/electrical rooms, blocked aisles, and general clutter. Rate using the 5-point scale and provide specific observations in the notes field.

Minimum Standards: Blocked exits, non-functional exit signs, and missing fire extinguishers in required locations are minimum standards violations. These must be documented as High Priority recommendations regardless of other risk quality factors.
Recommendations RECS

Recommendations are the actionable output of the inspection. They must be specific, measurable, and prioritized. Vague recommendations are not useful to the insured or the underwriter.

Priority Levels

High Priority: Conditions that represent an immediate or serious risk of loss, or that violate minimum underwriting standards. Typically require correction within 30–60 days. Failure to comply may result in coverage restrictions or cancellation.

Medium Priority: Conditions that elevate risk but are not immediately critical. Typically require correction within 90–180 days.

Low Priority / Advisory: Best practice recommendations or minor improvements. No specific compliance deadline but should be communicated to the insured.

Writing Effective Recommendations

Poor: "Improve housekeeping."

Better: "Remove accumulated combustible waste (cardboard, wood scraps) from the northeast storage room. Implement a scheduled weekly waste removal program. Combustible waste accumulation observed at time of inspection presents a significant fire load adjacent to the electrical panel."

Each recommendation should identify: the specific deficiency, its location, the required corrective action, and the reason it is required.

Photo Documentation PHOTOS

Photographs provide objective evidence that supports your written findings. A well-documented photo log significantly enhances the credibility and utility of the inspection report.

Required Photo Coverage

At minimum, capture: all four exterior elevations, the main entrance, the roof (if safely accessible), the electrical panel, the sprinkler riser (if applicable), the fire alarm panel, any significant hazards or deficiencies, and representative interior views of each major occupancy area.

Bulk Loading

Use the 📁 Bulk Load Photos button to select multiple images at once from your device. Each image is automatically assigned to its own numbered slot. Assign a category and caption to each photo after loading. The first photo loaded becomes the cover page photo for the formal report.

Photo Quality: Ensure photos are in focus, well-lit, and show the relevant detail clearly. For deficiency photos, take both a wide-angle context shot and a close-up detail shot. Add descriptive captions — a photo without context is of limited value to the underwriter reviewing the report remotely.
Underwriter Summary SUMMARY

The Summary section is your professional assessment of the overall risk. The underwriter will read this section first. Write clearly and confidently.

Risk Quality Assessment

Preferred / Low: Well-maintained property, superior construction, comprehensive fire protection, no significant hazards, excellent housekeeping, favourable loss history.

Acceptable / Standard: Average condition, standard construction, adequate fire protection, minor hazards controlled, satisfactory housekeeping, normal loss history.

Moderate: Some concerns — deferred maintenance, moderate hazards, fire protection gaps, poor housekeeping, adverse loss frequency or severity. Recommendations required.

High Risk / Refer: Significant deficiencies, high hazard occupancy, inadequate fire protection, very poor housekeeping, significant loss history. Requires underwriter referral before binding.

Decline: Conditions that fall outside acceptable risk appetite regardless of premium. Examples: severe structural deficiency, imminent collapse risk, illegal occupancy, evidence of fraud.

Underwriting Recommendation

Your recommendation should be consistent with your risk quality rating and the recommendations listed. If you rate the risk as Moderate but recommend "Accept as is," the underwriter will question the inconsistency. Ensure alignment between: Risk Quality, number and priority of recommendations, and UW Recommendation.

Cooking / Open Flame
Commercial cooking operations, grease management, suppression systems
NFPA 96NFPA 13 · NFPA 10
Why It Matters

Cooking operations are among the leading causes of commercial property fires. Grease accumulation in hoods, ducts, and on cooking equipment is highly combustible and can cause rapid fire spread through the entire ventilation system. Deep fryer fires can reach 1,000°F within seconds.

Key Inspection Points
  • Hood suppression system type (UL 300 required for modern installations)
  • Service tag — semi-annual service required for most commercial operations
  • Hood and duct cleaning frequency and records
  • Grease accumulation observed at time of inspection
  • Cooking equipment arrangement and clearances
  • Staff training on system use and manual pull station
Grease Accumulation Assessment
  • Minimal: Clean hood and filters, no visible grease buildup — good housekeeping
  • Moderate: Some grease on filters and hood interior — cleaning overdue but not critical
  • Heavy: Significant grease buildup on duct surfaces, plenum, or fan — immediate cleaning required. Issue High Priority recommendation.
  • Critical: Dripping grease, visible ignition risk — recommend shutdown until cleaned
UW Signals
  • ✓ UL 300 system, current service, regular cleaning → Standard
  • ~ Older system, service current, minor grease → Monitor
  • ! Heavy grease, overdue service → High Priority Rec
  • ✗ No suppression, heavy grease, open flame → Refer / Decline
Solid Fuel Cooking: Wood-fired ovens, charcoal grills, and smokers present significantly elevated risk compared to gas or electric cooking. These require spark arrestors, non-combustible ash disposal, and enhanced clearances. Always flag for underwriter review.

Welding / Cutting Operations
Hot work hazards, spark control, hot work permit programs
NFPA 51BNFPA 51 · NFPA 10
Why It Matters

Hot work (welding, cutting, brazing, grinding) is a leading cause of industrial fires. Sparks and spatter can travel up to 35 feet and ignite combustibles hours after work is completed. Many major warehouse and industrial fires are attributed to hot work with inadequate controls.

Hot Work Permit Program Elements
  • Written permit issued before each hot work operation
  • Pre-work inspection of the area — 35-ft radius of combustibles cleared
  • Fire watch posted during work AND for minimum 30 minutes after completion
  • Fire extinguisher at the work site
  • Supervisory sign-off required
  • Contractor compliance with the permit program
Welding Types — Risk Hierarchy
  • MIG/TIG: Lower spatter, more controlled — lower risk
  • Stick/Arc: More spatter and slag — moderate risk
  • Oxy-acetylene cutting: High spatter, acetylene flammable — elevated risk
  • Plasma cutting: Hot metal particles, UV radiation — elevated risk
  • Grinding: Often overlooked — produces hot sparks that can travel significant distances
Designated vs. Portable Operations

Dedicated welding booth: Permanent area with non-combustible walls, concrete floor, ventilation — strongly preferred. Limits exposure to defined area.

Portable/roving operations: High risk — hot work occurs throughout the facility in proximity to varying combustible loads. Hot work permit program is essential.

Contractor Hot Work: Many losses occur when contractors (plumbers, HVAC technicians) perform hot work without the property owner's awareness or controls. Confirm whether the hot work permit program applies to contractors and whether it is enforced.

Spray Painting / Coating
Flammable vapour control, spray booths, electrostatic ignition
NFPA 33NFPA 30 · NFPA 13
Why It Matters

Solvent-based spray coatings create flammable vapour clouds that can ignite from static electricity, electrical equipment, or any open flame. The Lower Explosive Limit (LEL) can be reached quickly in enclosed spaces. Spray booth fires can involve rapid fire development with intense heat.

Spray Booth Requirements
  • Listed (FM/UL approved) construction — non-combustible panels
  • Exhaust fan with explosion-proof motor
  • Filters: maintained and changed regularly — clogged filters are a fire hazard
  • Electrical: all fixtures inside booth must be explosion-proof rated
  • Fire suppression: dry chemical system inside booth and at air intake
  • Interlocks: exhaust must run before and after spraying
Overspray Accumulation

Overspray deposits on booth walls, filters, ducts, and exhaust fans are highly combustible. Heavy accumulation can self-ignite from friction heat in the exhaust fan. Assess the accumulation level honestly — the interior of booth walls and duct surfaces should be checked.

Residue on external surfaces (roof, walls outside booth) indicates exhaust filter failure.

Water-Based vs. Solvent-Based

Water-based coatings: Significantly lower fire risk. No LEL concern during normal application. Still require ventilation but much preferred from an insurance perspective.

Solvent-based/flammable: Full spray booth and suppression requirements apply. Storage of solvents must be in approved flammable storage cabinets away from the booth.

Open Area Spraying: Spraying outside of a listed spray booth using flammable materials is a serious deficiency. Issue a High Priority recommendation for either the installation of a proper booth or the conversion to water-based coatings. This condition often falls outside standard underwriting appetite.

Chemical Mixing
Chemical compatibility, SDS compliance, containment, reactivity hazards
NFPA 400NFPA 30 · NFPA 704
Why It Matters

Chemical mixing operations present multiple simultaneous hazards: fire (flammable solvents), explosion (reactive compounds), toxicity (inhalation/skin contact), and environmental (spills). Incompatible chemicals stored or mixed together can cause violent reactions, toxic gas generation, or spontaneous ignition.

Chemical Compatibility Rules
  • Acids + Bases: Exothermic reaction, may generate heat and fumes
  • Oxidizers + Flammables: Can cause spontaneous ignition or explosion — must be separated by minimum 20 ft or fire-rated wall
  • Acids + Cyanides/Sulfides: Generate toxic hydrogen cyanide or hydrogen sulfide gas
  • Bleach + Ammonia: Generates chloramine vapour — toxic
  • Storage must segregate incompatible chemical classes
SDS / WHMIS Assessment

Safety Data Sheets must be: current (SDS format per GHS standard), readily accessible to workers, and covering all hazardous materials on site. Ask to see the SDS binder. Absence of SDS for chemicals present is a significant compliance deficiency and a High Priority recommendation.

The SDS Section 2 (Hazard Identification) and Section 7 (Storage) are most relevant for insurance purposes.

Secondary Containment

Required for: any quantity of acids, corrosives, or flammable liquids stored in bulk containers. Containment capacity must equal 110% of the largest single container or 10% of total storage volume, whichever is greater.

Inspect for: cracks in berm/pallet, drain valves that are open (defeats containment), and incompatible materials within same containment area.

Regulatory Compliance: Chemical storage above threshold quantities triggers regulatory reporting requirements under EPCRA (US) or Ontario Reg. 675/98 (Canada). Non-compliance with environmental regulations can affect insurability and create pollution liability exposure beyond standard property policy coverage.

Woodworking / Combustible Dust
Dust explosion hazards, deflagration risk, dust control systems
NFPA 664NFPA 654 · NFPA 91 · NFPA 33
Dust Explosion Fundamentals

Combustible dust explosions require five elements (the "Dust Explosion Pentagon"): fuel (dust), oxygen, ignition source, suspension in air, and confinement. Wood dust, at concentrations above its LEL, can deflagrate (rapid burning) or detonate (explosive shockwave). A primary explosion (small) can dislodge settled dust causing a catastrophic secondary explosion.

Dust Accumulation Assessment
  • 1/32 inch (0.8mm): NFPA 654 threshold for housekeeping action — any visible layer of dust on horizontal surfaces
  • Check: roof trusses, beams, ledges, top of equipment, electrical conduit, light fixtures
  • Fine dust on elevated surfaces is more dangerous than coarse shavings on floors
  • Heavy accumulation on beams requires mechanical removal — compressed air blowdown simply redistributes dust into the air
Dust Collection Systems

Adequate system: Central dust collector with spark detection/extinguishment, properly sized ductwork, interlocked with machinery, and regularly serviced with clean filters.

Inadequate: Portable collectors on individual machines only, no spark protection, overloaded filter bags, or collector located inside the building without explosion relief venting.

Ask for service records. Overloaded filter bags can auto-ignite from heat.

Ignition Source Controls
  • All electrical equipment in the dust area should be dust-rated (NEMA 12 enclosures minimum)
  • Metal tramp (nails, screws in wood) can cause sparks in cutting equipment — magnetic separators at infeed
  • Static bonding/grounding on all ductwork sections
  • No smoking policy rigorously enforced
  • Hot work requires dust area clearance and extra fire watch duration
Finishing Operations: The combination of wood dust AND solvent-based finishes (lacquers, stains) creates a compounded hazard. Finishing areas must be physically separated from woodworking areas, and finishing operations should cease before cutting/sanding resumes. This combination typically results in Extra Hazard Group 2 classification.
🫙
Compressed Gas
Cylinder storage, BLEVE risk, fuel/oxidizer separation
NFPA 55NFPA 51 · NFPA 58 (LP-Gas)
Why It Matters

Compressed gas cylinders present dual hazards: the physical hazard of a high-pressure vessel (a falling unsecured cylinder can become a missile if the valve breaks off) and the chemical hazard of the gas itself (flammable, toxic, or asphyxiant). A BLEVE (Boiling Liquid Expanding Vapour Explosion) from a heated propane cylinder is catastrophic.

Fuel/Oxidizer Separation Requirements

Flammable gases (propane, acetylene, hydrogen, natural gas) must be stored separately from oxidizing gases (oxygen) by either:

  • Minimum 20 feet (6m) separation, OR
  • A non-combustible partition at least 5 feet high with minimum 0.5-hour fire rating

The reaction between acetylene and oxygen (oxy-acetylene) in a fire can be particularly violent. Never store them in the same room.

Cylinder Securement

All cylinders must be chained or strapped to a wall, post, or cylinder cart. Two-point restraint is preferred for tall cylinders. Inspect for:

  • Cylinders standing unsecured — immediate High Priority recommendation
  • Valve protection caps — present when not in use
  • Cylinders lying on their side (except acetylene — must always be upright)
  • Evidence of corrosion on cylinder body or valve
Storage Location
  • Outdoors preferred for flammable gas cylinders whenever climate permits
  • If indoors: dedicated ventilated room, no ignition sources, no basements (heavier-than-air gases accumulate at low points)
  • Away from heat sources, electrical panels, and welding operations
  • Signage: FLAMMABLE GAS / NO SMOKING / NO OPEN FLAMES
  • Propane: outdoor storage preferred; indoor storage in listed propane storage room only
Acetylene Special Note: Acetylene is uniquely dangerous — it is shock-sensitive above 15 psig and must always be stored and used in the upright position. Acetylene cylinders stored on their side must be stood upright and allowed to stabilize for one hour before use. Note any horizontal acetylene cylinders as a significant deficiency.
🛢️
Flammable & Combustible Liquid Storage
Flash point classifications, storage requirements, spill control
NFPA 30NFPA 30A · NFPA 13 · NFPA 400
Flash Point Classification
  • Class IA: FP <73°F, BP <100°F — Diethyl ether, pentane. Extreme fire hazard.
  • Class IB: FP <73°F, BP ≥100°F — Gasoline, acetone, methanol. Most common industrial flammables.
  • Class IC: FP 73–100°F — Xylene, lacquer thinner. Still highly flammable.
  • Class II: FP 100–140°F — Diesel, stoddard solvent. Combustible but less volatile.
  • Class IIIA: FP 140–200°F — Fuel oils, lubricating oils. Lower volatility.
  • Class IIIB: FP ≥200°F — Motor oils, cooking oils. Limited vapour at room temperature.
Maximum Allowable Quantities

NFPA 30 sets maximum allowable quantities (MAQ) without requiring a flammable storage room. For Class IB liquids, the typical MAQ inside a building is 120 gallons (approx. 450L) per control area without a sprinkler system. Exceeding MAQ requires: a dedicated flammable storage room with 2-hour fire rating, or an approved flammable liquid storage building.

Always compare observed quantities to the applicable MAQ and document any exceedance.

Flammable Storage Cabinet Requirements
  • Listed FM/UL 1275 or ULC/ORD-C30 construction
  • Self-closing doors — never prop open
  • Maximum 60 gallons per cabinet
  • Maximum 3 cabinets per fire area without engineered exception
  • Venting: cabinets do not require venting — if vented, must exhaust to safe exterior location
  • Location: away from ignition sources and exit paths
Bonding & Grounding

Static electricity generated during transfer of flammable liquids between containers can cause ignition. Bonding connects the containers together to equalize charge. Grounding dissipates charge to earth. Both are required when transferring Class I liquids.

Inspect for: bonding cables with alligator clips at dispensing stations, ground rod or connection to building ground system. Absent bonding/grounding for Class I liquid transfer is a High Priority recommendation.

Gasoline Storage: Gasoline (Class IB) is one of the most commonly encountered and most dangerous flammable liquids. Even a small quantity (1 gallon) can generate enough vapour to fill a room to explosive concentration. Indoor gasoline storage above consumer quantities (1 gallon/4L) without proper controls is a High Priority deficiency at minimum.
🧊
Cryogenic Materials
Oxygen deficiency, pressure buildup, boiloff management
NFPA 55NFPA 50 · CGA P-12
Why It Matters

Cryogenic liquids (stored at temperatures below -150°C / -238°F) present severe hazards: asphyxiation from oxygen displacement (liquid nitrogen expanding 700:1 in volume to gas), pressure buildup causing vessel rupture, contact burns colder than dry ice, and in the case of liquid oxygen — extreme fire enhancement (oxidizer).

Oxygen Deficiency Hazard

Normal oxygen concentration: 20.9%. Hazard thresholds:

  • 19.5%: OSHA minimum — below this is oxygen-deficient atmosphere
  • 16%: Impaired judgment, rapid heart rate
  • 14%: Faulty judgment, extreme fatigue
  • 6%: Convulsions, death within minutes

A single large Dewar of liquid nitrogen spilling in an enclosed room can instantly create a lethal atmosphere. Continuous monitoring with alarming O2 sensors is essential.

Liquid Oxygen (LOX) Special Hazards

Liquid oxygen is an extremely powerful oxidizer. Contact with organic materials (clothing, oil, grease) that would normally be non-flammable can cause violent ignition. LOX-saturated materials can explode on impact.

  • No oil or grease anywhere near LOX equipment — use only LOX-compatible lubricants
  • Personnel must wear clean, oil-free clothing when handling LOX
  • LOX storage must be away from all combustible materials
  • Any LOX spill: evacuate, ventilate, wait for all frost to evaporate before re-entry
Pressure Relief & Venting

All cryogenic vessels have pressure relief valves that vent gas (boiloff) as the liquid warms. This gas must be safely vented to the exterior. Inspect for:

  • Relief valve vent termination — must go to safe exterior location, not into occupied space
  • Frost on exterior of vessel at non-insulated points — indicates insulation failure
  • Over-pressure buildup — safety relief may have been tampered with (dangerous)
  • Indoor placement without adequate ventilation for boiloff
Entry into Cryogen Storage Areas: Never enter an enclosed cryogen storage room alone or without first checking O2 levels with a calibrated monitor. If an O2 monitor is not available, treat the space as potentially oxygen-deficient. This applies to inspectors as well as workers.
🖨️
Printing & Ink Operations
Solvent vapours, rag accumulation, static discharge, UV curing
NFPA 30NFPA 86 · NFPA 654 · NFPA 33
Why It Matters

Commercial printing operations using solvent-based inks generate flammable vapours during printing, press cleaning, and solvent evaporation from wet prints. Ink-soaked rags and wipers are prone to spontaneous combustion. Large printing operations have significant fire load from paper stock, which burns intensely and is difficult to extinguish.

Printing Process Risk Assessment
  • Digital/Laser/Inkjet: Minimal solvent hazard. Toner fires possible from paper jams with heat. Lower risk.
  • Offset Lithographic: Isopropyl alcohol fountain solution. Blanket wash solvents (flammable). Moderate risk.
  • Screen Printing: Solvent-based inks common. Flash cure units (heat). Moderate-high risk.
  • Flexographic/Gravure: Often use solvent-based inks. Significant vapour generation at high production volumes. High risk.
  • UV Curing: UV inks not flammable, but UV lamps generate ozone. Lower fire risk but check lamp cooling and ballast condition.
Rag / Wiper Management

Ink-soaked rags containing certain drying oils and solvents can self-heat through oxidation and spontaneously ignite. This is a well-documented cause of fires in printing operations.

  • Soiled rags must be stored in listed self-closing metal waste containers — never plastic bags or open boxes
  • Containers must be emptied daily to an outside dumpster away from the building
  • Rag laundering on site? Verify equipment and controls for washer/dryer fires
  • Ask about end-of-shift rag disposal — lack of procedure is a High Priority recommendation
Paper Stock Hazard

Large printing plants may store hundreds of tons of paper in roll or sheet form. Paper is an ordinary combustible but burns intensely and has a very high surface area. Key concerns:

  • Sprinkler system adequacy for paper storage height and density
  • Housekeeping — paper trim, dust accumulation
  • Forklift battery charging areas (hydrogen off-gassing)
  • Smoking controls — paper dust can be a secondary fuel
Static Control: High-speed web presses generate significant static electricity from paper-metal contact. Static discharge can ignite solvent vapours. Check for anti-static brushes or bars on press, grounding straps on all press units, and humidity controls (higher humidity reduces static buildup). Inadequate static control combined with solvent-based inks is a significant hazard.

ISO Construction Classification Guide CONSTRUCTION

The six-class ISO/insurance construction scheme is the foundation of commercial property rating — it determines base rates, replacement cost expectations, and fire spread potential. Classification depends on the structural frame and load-bearing elements, not the exterior finish or cladding — a common source of misclassification. This guide expands on the summary in the Form Filling Guide (Construction section) with fuller definitions, Canadian examples, and field identification tips for both finished buildings and buildings under construction.

The #1 Misclassification Trap: Exterior cladding is not the structural frame. A building with brick veneer, EIFS (stucco-look synthetic panels), or metal siding can be hiding a wood-frame, steel-frame, or masonry structure behind it. Always identify what actually bears the load and supports the roof/floor — not what you see on the outside walls. When in doubt, check exposed structural elements in a mechanical room, unfinished basement, attic access, or loading dock.

🪵
Class 1 — Frame
Wood or other combustible structural frame, walls, floor, and roof

Definition: Exterior walls, floor, and roof structural members are wood or other combustible material — regardless of exterior cladding. Brick veneer or vinyl siding over a wood-stud frame is still Class 1.

Canadian Examples: Single-family homes, wood-frame low-rise apartments/condos (up to 6 storeys under current wood-frame building code provisions in most provinces), older rural commercial buildings, pole-barn agricultural structures, small strip retail built before 1980.

Field ID — Finished Building
  • Check an attic access, unfinished basement, or utility room for exposed dimensional lumber (2×4, 2×6, 2×8) or wood roof trusses
  • Brick veneer trap: tap the wall — veneer sounds hollow with a visible weep-hole/air-gap line near the base; true masonry does not
  • Interior partition walls with visible wood studs (renovation openings, unfinished storage areas) confirm wood frame
Under Construction — What to Look For
  • Exposed wood studs and OSB/plywood sheathing before cladding is installed
  • Wood roof trusses being craned/placed, visible before roof covering
  • House-wrap or building paper over visible stud bays
🧱
Class 2 — Joisted Masonry
Masonry load-bearing walls, wood or combustible floor/roof

Definition: Exterior bearing walls of masonry (brick, concrete block, stone) that carry the structural load; floor and roof structure is wood or other combustible material.

Canadian Examples: Older downtown "Main Street" commercial storefronts, pre-1960s walk-up apartment buildings, older schoolhouses and churches, warehouses with masonry bearing walls and wood-truss roofs.

Field ID — Finished Building
  • Confirm the masonry is load-bearing, not a veneer — check wall thickness at a window or door opening (solid masonry typically 8"+ vs. 3–4" thin veneer)
  • Check the roofline/parapet — true masonry continues as the structural wall, not stopping short with a different material behind it
  • Attic or roof-space access shows wood joists, trusses, or purlins resting directly on the masonry walls
Under Construction — What to Look For
  • Masonry block/brick walls being laid course by course, often with visible mortar joints and scaffolding
  • Wood roof trusses being placed on top of completed masonry walls
  • Wood floor joists set into masonry wall pockets
🏭
Class 3 — Non-Combustible
Unprotected steel frame with metal deck and non-combustible cladding

Definition: Exterior walls, floor, and roof of metal or other non-combustible material supported by an unprotected structural steel frame — no fire-resistance rating on the steel itself.

Canadian Examples: Pre-engineered metal buildings (PEMBs), distribution centres and warehouses, light industrial/manufacturing buildings, big-box retail, agricultural equipment buildings, many strip malls with steel frame and metal panel/EIFS cladding.

Field ID — Finished Building
  • Confirm the primary structural frame is steel — exposed I-beams, tube steel columns, or rigid frames are typically visible in warehouse interiors, loading docks, or utility rooms
  • Corrugated metal deck roof (visible from below in an open warehouse ceiling) is a strong indicator
  • No spray-applied fireproofing visible on the steel — if a grey/white sprayed or troweled coating fully encases beams and columns, reclassify toward Class 5/6
Under Construction — What to Look For
  • The clearest class to identify during construction — exposed red-primed or galvanized steel columns and rigid frames fully visible before wall panels go up
  • Corrugated metal roof deck being installed before insulation/membrane
  • Bolted or welded steel connections visible at eave and ridge lines
🏢
Class 4 — Masonry Non-Combustible
Masonry load-bearing walls with non-combustible floor/roof

Definition: Exterior bearing walls of masonry as in Class 2, but the floor and roof structure is non-combustible (metal deck on steel bar joists, concrete) rather than wood.

Canadian Examples: Masonry-walled warehouses/industrial buildings with metal roof decking, big-box retail with CMU perimeter walls and steel bar-joist roofs, mid-century industrial buildings retrofitted from wood to steel roof framing.

Field ID — Finished Building
  • Confirm load-bearing masonry walls exactly as in Class 2 (thickness, continuity to parapet)
  • Ceiling/roof space shows open-web steel bar joists and metal deck rather than wood trusses — this is the key distinction from Class 2
Under Construction — What to Look For
  • Masonry walls being built up alongside steel bar joists and metal decking being placed for the roof — a visual hybrid of Class 2 and Class 3 techniques
  • Steel joists visibly resting on completed masonry wall bond beams
🏗️
Class 5 — Modified Fire Resistive
Rated concrete/masonry assemblies, 1–2 hr fire resistance (not full Class 6)

Definition: Floor and roof assemblies of masonry or reinforced concrete providing a fire-resistance rating of 1–2 hours, but not meeting the full 2-hour rating on all structural elements required for Class 6.

Canadian Examples: Concrete tilt-up panel buildings (very common in Western Canada industrial parks — Surrey, Langley, Calgary, Edmonton), precast concrete panel construction with steel roof framing, some mid-rise concrete/steel hybrid buildings.

Field ID — Finished Building
  • Tilt-up concrete: large, flat wall panels with a distinctive cast texture and visible vertical joint/reveal lines every 20–30 ft where individual panels meet
  • Check whether the roof structure is fire-rated (precast plank) or an unrated lighter steel joist/deck system on top of the concrete walls — the classic Class 5 combination is concrete walls (fire resistant) with an unrated steel roof
Under Construction — What to Look For
  • Highly distinctive: large flat concrete panels cast horizontally on the ground/floor slab, then tilt-lifted into vertical position by crane — a very recognizable construction technique if photographed during the tilt-up phase
  • Exposed rebar and panel forms before pour
  • Steel roof joists being placed on top of completed tilt-up walls
🏙️
Class 6 — Fire Resistive
All structural elements minimum 2-hour fire-resistance rating

Definition: All structural elements — walls, floors, roof, and structural frame — carry a minimum 2-hour fire-resistance rating, typically via reinforced concrete construction or fully fireproofed structural steel.

Canadian Examples: High-rise office towers, high-rise residential condominiums, hospitals, parkades, major institutional buildings.

Field ID — Finished Building
  • Poured-in-place or precast concrete structural frame (columns, beams, floor slabs) is the most common method
  • If steel-framed, look for spray-applied or board fireproofing fully encasing all beams and columns — a thick grey/white coating, sometimes concealed behind a drywall enclosure
Under Construction — What to Look For
  • Rebar cages and concrete formwork being assembled floor by floor (climbing/jump forms), concrete pump trucks on site
  • For steel high-rises: fireproofing being spray-applied to the exposed structural steel frame before other finishes are installed
Mixed / Combination Construction: Many buildings combine construction types — e.g. a masonry front facade on an otherwise steel-frame building, or a concrete podium under wood-frame upper storeys. Use the percentage breakdown fields in the inspection form for buildings with more than one construction type, and assign the ISO class reflecting the predominant construction of the insured building. When a single occupancy unit within a larger building has different construction than its neighbours (e.g. a masonry firewall separation), note this explicitly — it materially affects fire spread potential.
Building Photos Are the Best Reference: Over time, this section can be supplemented with your own inspection photographs of confirmed construction types — a real Class 3 pre-engineered steel building under construction, a real Class 5 tilt-up pour, and so on, are more valuable field references than generic stock imagery. Consider flagging especially clear examples encountered in the field for addition here.
Sprinkler Systems & NFPA 13 NFPA 13

Beyond the design density/area criteria already covered under NFPA Hazard Classification, the type of sprinkler system installed materially affects both fire performance and underwriting judgment. This guide covers the four core system types, special application systems for storage, plastics manufacturing as an occupancy hazard class, and high-piled storage definitions for plastic commodities — none of which were covered in the earlier NFPA Hazard Classification table.

System TypePipe ContentsResponse SpeedTypical Use
Wet PipeWater, at all timesFastest — immediate dischargeHeated spaces above freezing, most common system type
Dry PipePressurized air/nitrogen; water held back by a dry pipe valveDelayed 30–90+ sec while air vents and water travelsUnheated spaces subject to freezing — parking garages, loading docks, attics, cold storage
Pre-ActionAir (normally atmospheric or supervisory pressure); requires separate detection to release water into the pipe before a sprinkler can dischargeSlower — two events must occur (detection, then heat at head)Water-sensitive contents — data centres, archives, museums, freezers
DelugeOpen (non-fusible) sprinklers, empty pipe; water released to ALL heads simultaneously by a separate detection systemFast, total-area discharge — no waiting for individual heads to fuseRapid fire spread hazards — aircraft hangars, flammable liquid areas, transformer protection
💧
Wet Pipe System
Water-filled piping at all times — the simplest, fastest, and most common system type
How It Works

Piping is charged with water under pressure at all times. When a sprinkler head's heat-sensitive element fuses, water discharges immediately from that head only. No detection system or valve sequence is required — this is the fastest-responding and most reliable system type, and the default choice wherever freezing is not a concern.

Field Identification
  • Alarm check valve (not a dry pipe valve) at the riser — a simpler valve body without an air pressure gauge on the system side
  • No air compressor or nitrogen generator connected to the riser
  • Standard closed-head sprinklers (fusible link or glass bulb) at the ceiling
Underwriting Considerations
  • Preferred system type where the space is reliably heated above freezing — fastest response, simplest maintenance
  • Not appropriate for unheated spaces — a frozen wet pipe system can fracture and cause severe water damage, or fail to operate
  • Check heating reliability in marginal spaces (unheated additions, mezzanines, loading docks) — a wet system in a space that periodically drops below freezing is a real exposure
🌬️
Dry Pipe System
Pressurized air holds water back — for unheated spaces subject to freezing
How It Works

Piping is charged with pressurized air (or nitrogen) instead of water. When a sprinkler head fuses, air escapes, tripping the dry pipe valve at the riser and allowing water to flow into the piping and out the open head. This introduces a real delay — typically 30 to 90+ seconds depending on system size — between head activation and actual water discharge, since air must vent and water must travel the full length of the piping first.

Field Identification
  • Dry pipe valve assembly at the riser with an air pressure gauge on the system side, separate from the water supply gauge
  • Air compressor or nitrogen generator connected to maintain system air pressure
  • Common in unheated garages, loading docks, attics, unheated warehouses, and refrigerated/cold storage areas
Underwriting Considerations
  • The discharge delay is a genuine fire-growth consideration in fast-developing fires — this is a real trade-off accepted in exchange for freeze protection, not a deficiency by itself
  • Larger dry systems (long trip time) may require calculation per NFPA 13 to confirm trip time is within limits — ask about system size if unusually large
  • Low-point drains must be properly maintained — accumulated water in dry piping is a common cause of pipe corrosion, and can also cause false trips or reduced air holding capacity
Canadian Climate Relevance: Dry pipe systems are especially common across Canada in any unheated or marginally-heated space — attics, unheated loading areas, parking structures, seasonal buildings. Confirm the air compressor is functional and the system is holding pressure; a dry system that has lost air pressure will trip immediately on any head fusing, behaving like an unintended wet system with no freeze protection.
🛡️
Pre-Action System
Requires separate detection before water enters the piping — for water-sensitive contents
How It Works

Piping normally holds air (at atmospheric or supervisory pressure) and a separate fire detection system (smoke or heat detectors) must activate before the pre-action valve releases water into the piping. Only after that does the system behave like a wet system — an individual head must still fuse from heat to actually discharge. This double-event requirement (detection, then heat) protects against accidental water discharge from a damaged pipe or head, since air alone escaping doesn't release water — a real fire condition must be detected first.

Single vs. double interlock: Single-interlock systems fill with water on detection alone, then behave like a wet system. Double-interlock systems require BOTH detection AND head fusion before water is released — the highest protection against accidental discharge, common in data centres and archives.

Field Identification
  • Pre-action valve assembly at the riser, similar in appearance to a dry pipe valve but paired with a fire alarm/detection control panel
  • Smoke or heat detectors visible in the protected space, in addition to the sprinkler heads themselves
  • Common in server rooms, data centres, museums, archives, and other high-value, water-sensitive occupancies
Underwriting Considerations
  • Confirm the detection system is tested and monitored — a pre-action system depends entirely on its detection component working correctly, more so than wet or dry systems
  • Double-interlock systems carry the longest potential delay to water discharge of the four types — appropriate where accidental discharge risk (to contents) outweighs the slower response, not appropriate for general life-safety-driven occupancies
🌊
Deluge System
Open sprinklers, total-area discharge — for extreme, rapid fire spread hazards
How It Works

Unlike the other three types, deluge systems use open sprinkler heads (no individual heat-fusible element) connected to empty piping. A separate fire detection system triggers the deluge valve, releasing water to all heads in the system simultaneously — the entire protected area is deluged at once, rather than only the heads nearest the fire. This is used where fire can spread faster than individual head fusion could respond in time.

Field Identification
  • Sprinkler heads have no visible fusible link or glass bulb — they are simply open orifices
  • Deluge valve at the riser paired with a dedicated detection system (heat, flame, or linear detection)
  • Common in aircraft hangars, flammable liquid loading/processing areas, transformer/switchgear protection, and conveyor galleries in high-hazard processes
Underwriting Considerations
  • Reserved for genuinely extreme hazards — presence of a deluge system is itself a signal of elevated underlying hazard, not a mitigant to be taken at face value
  • Requires the largest water supply of the four types, since all heads flow simultaneously — confirm water supply adequacy was engineered for full simultaneous demand
Special Application Systems for Storage: High-piled and rack storage occupancies often use specially engineered system types beyond the four core types above — ESFR (Early Suppression Fast Response) and CMSA (Control Mode Specific Application) sprinklers, designed to suppress rather than merely control a fire in tall storage arrays. These require specialist design per NFPA 13 Chapters 20–24 — see the High-Piled Storage & Plastics note below, and the Storage/Plastics row in the NFPA Hazard Classification table.
🏭
Plastics Manufacturing & Processing — Occupancy Classification
Not covered in the general NFPA Hazard Classification table — addressed separately here
Classification

Plastics manufacturing is explicitly listed by NFPA 13 as an example Extra Hazard Group 2 occupancy — the classification applies to the manufacturing/processing operation itself (injection molding, extrusion, blow molding, thermoforming, foam manufacturing), separate from any question of how finished plastic goods are later stored (see High-Piled Storage below).

Why Extra Hazard 2
  • Injection molding and extrusion equipment uses hydraulic oil under significant pressure — a hydraulic line failure near a hot mold/barrel is a recognized ignition scenario
  • Molten plastic and heated barrels/dies operate at high temperatures (commonly 350–600°F depending on resin) — an uncontrolled melt or barrel failure presents a direct ignition source
  • Many processes involve flammable/combustible solvents or coatings (mold release agents, secondary finishing, adhesive bonding, solvent cleaning) consistent with the "moderate to substantial flammable/combustible liquids" definition of EH2
  • Foam/expanded plastic manufacturing carries an especially high heat release rate once ignited
  • Burning plastics generally have a very high heat release rate and produce dense, toxic smoke — a fire in a plastics plant develops and spreads faster than the equivalent fire load in most other occupancies
Key Inspection Points
  • Type of process — injection molding, extrusion, blow molding, thermoforming, rotational molding, foam manufacturing
  • Hydraulic fluid type, quantity, and condition of hydraulic lines near hot equipment
  • Resin/pellet storage — quantity, silo vs. bag storage, proximity to processing equipment and ignition sources
  • Presence of flammable solvents, mold release agents, or adhesives, and their storage/handling
  • Dust collection if any grinding/regrind operation is present (recycled regrind dust can itself be a combustible dust hazard)
  • Housekeeping around processing equipment — accumulated regrind, trim scrap, or finished parts near heat sources
Distinct from Storage Classification: Do not conflate the occupancy classification (the manufacturing process itself, typically EH2) with the commodity/storage classification of the plastic products once made. A plastics manufacturing floor is EH2 by process; a separate finished-goods warehouse storing the same company's plastic products is classified by the High-Piled Storage / Group A-B-C commodity rules below — these are two different questions, both potentially applicable in a single facility with a manufacturing area and an attached warehouse.
📦
High-Piled Storage & Plastics Definitions
When storage height triggers additional fire protection requirements, and what Group A/B/C plastics means
High-Piled Storage — Height Thresholds
  • General combustible storage: top of storage greater than 12 ft in height (in closely packed piles, or on pallets/racks/shelves) triggers high-piled storage requirements under the fire code
  • Designated high-hazard commodities (rubber tires, Group A plastics, flammable liquids, idle pallets): threshold drops to greater than 6 ft in height under the fire code
  • NFPA 13 specifically applies its high-piled storage protection criteria to Group A plastics once stored over 5 ft in height — a lower, sprinkler-design-specific threshold distinct from the fire code's 6 ft trigger
Group A / B / C Plastics — What the Groups Mean
  • Plastics are grouped by heat of combustion — Group A is the highest hazard, Group B moderate, Group C lowest
  • Group C plastics (e.g. PVC, phenolics, melamine, urea formaldehyde) burn similarly to ordinary combustibles and are treated as a Class III commodity
  • Group B plastics (e.g. nylon, cellulosics, fluoroplastics) are treated as a Class IV commodity
  • Group A plastics (e.g. ABS, acrylic, polyethylene, polypropylene, polystyrene, polyurethane, PET) are the highest hazard, and are further subdivided as expanded (foamed) or unexpanded — expanded/foamed Group A plastics carry the greatest hazard of all due to very high surface area and rapid heat release
What This Means in the Field
  • Note the commodity — is it packaged (cartoned) or exposed (uncartoned)? Cartoned goods are somewhat less severe than the same commodity exposed
  • Note the pallet type — plastic pallets (other than polypropylene/HDPE tested types) can increase the effective commodity classification by up to two classes versus wood pallets
  • Note storage height and configuration — solid pile, palletized, single-row rack, double-row rack, or multi-row rack all have different protection implications at the same commodity class
  • Record these details precisely rather than assigning a class yourself — as noted in the NFPA Hazard Classification table, exact design adequacy for Group A/B/C plastics and high-piled storage requires review by a qualified fire protection engineer
This Is a Refer-to-Specialist Area: As established in the NFPA Hazard Classification table, exact sprinkler design adequacy for high-piled storage and Group A/B/C plastics depends on storage height, ceiling height, encapsulation, rack configuration, and sprinkler type (CMDA/CMSA/ESFR) per NFPA 13 Chapters 20–24. The definitions above are for recognizing when this applies during an inspection — not for determining design adequacy, which remains a specialist task.
FUS Grading Methodology FUS

Fire Underwriters Survey (FUS) — a Verisk Canada service, formerly Opta Information Intelligence — is the sole national organization providing standardized public fire protection grading for Canadian insurance purposes, used by over 90% of Canadian property and casualty insurers. Unlike a building-specific rating, FUS grades measure the community's fire protection infrastructure — fire department capability, water supply, and communications/dispatch — that a given property can rely on when a fire occurs. This directly complements the private (on-site) protection assessment already covered in the Form Filling Guide's Protection section.

Public Fire Protection Classification (PFPC)
1–10 scale — the grade that matters for commercial property inspection work
What It Measures

PFPC evaluates a community's ability to prevent and control major fires in commercial, industrial, institutional, and multi-family buildings — measured against a recognized standard, not against the community's actual fire loss history. A well-graded community may still experience fires; the grade reflects capability to respond, not a prediction of loss frequency.

Three major areas are assessed: Fire Department (apparatus, staffing, training, station distribution, response times), Water Supply (distribution system capacity and reliability to deliver adequate fire flow), and Communications (dispatch and alarm handling).

Insurer Tiering
  • Protected: approximately Class 1–5
  • Semi-Protected: approximately Class 5–8
  • Unprotected: approximately Class 9–10

Individual insurers may apply the tier boundaries slightly differently for their own underwriting appetite — treat these as general reference bands, not a fixed rule.

Response Distance Benchmarks
  • Maximum recognized response distance: 8 km by continuously maintained public road
  • 5 km or less is preferred, due to reduced response time
  • Private roads may be recognized where maintenance reliability is demonstrated
  • Rural areas without hydrant service may instead demonstrate tanker shuttle capability (minimum 900 L/min sustained for 2 hours) in the presence of an FUS representative
Field Application
  • Note the distance from the subject property to the nearest fire station/hall — this is a direct input to how FUS assesses that area, and is already something the Protection section of the inspection form asks you to record
  • A property just outside a municipality's protected boundary, or served by a volunteer/auxiliary department, may carry a materially different PFPC than a similar property downtown — don't assume municipal-wide uniformity
  • The current PFPC for a specific address can be looked up via the FUS Grading Index (fireunderwriters.ca) — when in doubt about a borderline or rural location, verify rather than assume
🏠
Dwelling Protection Grade (DPG)
1–5 scale — personal lines only, detached homes and duplexes
Scope

DPG applies only to detached one- and two-family dwellings (not larger multi-family buildings, which fall under PFPC). It measures the same underlying fire department capability as PFPC but benchmarked against the lower demand of a residential structure fire rather than a major commercial fire. DPG is derived from the same underlying PFPC survey data for the community.

Relevance to Commercial Inspection Work

DPG itself is not directly used for the commercial/COPE inspections this platform is built around — it's a personal lines grade. It's included here for completeness and because Sanket Risk Consultants' broader client base may occasionally ask about it in the context of a mixed-use or small multi-family property near the dwelling/commercial size threshold.

🔥
Canadian Wildfire Grading Index
A newer, separate FUS product addressing wildland-urban interface (WUI) exposure
What It Measures

Distinct from PFPC/DPG, this index assesses wildfire risk by zone based on: Hazard Severity (frequency/size of significant fires, climate factors), Exposure Severity (proportion of surrounding area with vegetation/fuel capable of producing radiant heat, short-range embers, and long-range embers), and a resulting Wildfire Risk Class combining both.

Field Application

Increasingly relevant for Canadian properties near forested or grassland urban-interface areas, particularly in BC, Alberta, and other wildfire-exposed regions. Where a property borders significant wildland vegetation, note this in the Exposure section of the inspection alongside standard exposure observations — this is a genuinely distinct risk pathway from the structural fire protection PFPC addresses.

Grading Is Not Static: Communities are reassessed roughly every five years via an FUS Community Outreach Questionnaire, and can request a new survey sooner if fire protection capability has materially improved (e.g. new water infrastructure, new fire hall). If FUS calculates a downgrade, the community is notified privately first and given up to 12 months to implement an improvement plan before the change is published — the previous grade remains in effect, marked "provisional," during that window. Grading reflects fire protection capability, not the community's actual fire loss history.
Practical Note: When inspecting a property in an unfamiliar or rural community, don't assume the PFPC based on the municipality's general reputation — verify the specific grade via the FUS Grading Index, particularly for properties near a protection boundary, served by a volunteer department, or in an area with recent fire service changes.
NBC 2025 Climatic Design Data NBC 2025

This platform's Sanket Nat-Cat Grading module draws directly on NBC 2025 Appendix C climatic and seismic design data — extracted for 680 Canadian locations and stored in the climatic_design_data lookup table used by the Natural Catastrophe Exposure section of the COPE form and the Nat-Cat Grading card in SCAN ONE. This guide explains what these values mean and documents what the platform already does with them, rather than introducing new functionality.

NBC 2025 Is the First Edition Built on Future Climate Projections: Previous NBC editions set design values from historical climate records alone. NBC 2025 instead incorporates climate change projections toward a Global Warming Level (GWL) of 2.5°C, referenced against a 1986–2016 baseline. This means a building designed and constructed under an earlier NBC edition may have been engineered to a materially lower snow, wind, or seismic demand than the current NBC 2025 value for that same location — a meaningful consideration when assessing the adequacy of an older structure, not just a bookkeeping change in the numbers.

Seismic Hazard — Smax
The seismic design parameter, as used in the platform's Nat-Cat Grading module
What Changed in NBC 2025

Earlier NBC editions (2015, 2020) used Sa(0.2) — spectral acceleration at 0.2 seconds — as the primary seismic design parameter referenced in Part 9 (housing and small buildings). NBC 2025 has replaced this with Smax. If you encounter older documentation, engineering reports, or platform notes referencing Sa(0.2), understand this as the predecessor to the Smax value now used.

Field Relevance
  • Higher Smax locations (much of coastal BC, parts of the St. Lawrence Valley/Quebec, and other known seismic zones) warrant closer attention to construction vulnerabilities: unreinforced masonry (Class 2/4 construction per the ISO Construction guide), soft-storey conditions (open ground floor with parking/retail below residential/office above), and older non-ductile concrete frames
  • Retrofit evidence (visible seismic bracing, base isolation, wall anchors/straps on masonry buildings) is a positive mitigating observation worth recording explicitly in higher Smax areas
❄️
Snow & Wind Design Loads
Ground snow load, rain load, and wind design pressure
Snow Load (Ss / Sr)

Total design snow load combines the ground snow load (Ss) with an associated rain-on-snow load component (Sr), adjusted by factors for roof exposure, accumulation (e.g. against parapets or adjacent taller structures), and — as of NBC 2025 — a thermal factor accounting for heat loss through the roof reducing retained snow depth. NBC 2025 also revised the underlying formula to reflect a "uniform risk" design approach across the country.

Wind Design Pressure

Reference wind pressure is used to determine structural and component/cladding design requirements — this governs the required strength of roof coverings, cladding attachment, glazing, and rooftop equipment anchorage. NBC 2025 shifted the governing structural wind and snow design toward longer return periods as part of the same uniform-risk approach.

Field Relevance
  • In high snow load regions, note roof structure condition carefully on older buildings — sagging purlins/joists, water ponding evidence, or visible roof structure reinforcement/sistering are meaningful observations
  • In high wind design pressure areas, note the roof covering type and apparent attachment method, condition of cladding/EIFS, and anchorage of rooftop mechanical equipment — loose or visibly inadequate anchorage is a wind-uplift concern worth flagging directly
  • A building's age relative to the code edition in force when it was built is directly relevant — an older building may not reflect current design values at all
How the Platform Uses This Data: The climatic_design_data table is queried by location (city + province) to retrieve the Smax, snow, and wind values for that specific location, feeding the Sanket Nat-Cat Grading module's demerit-weighted scoring for Earthquake, Wind/Storm, and Flood/Water hazard tiers — producing a Grade 1–5 rating consistent with the Property and Sprinkler grading methodology used elsewhere on the platform. If a lookup fails to find a location, this reflects a data or matching issue (see the platform's Debug Log panel) rather than an actual absence of climatic data for that area — every populated Canadian location has an applicable NBC value, even if the exact municipality name isn't in the 680-location table and a nearby larger centre must be used as a proxy.
This Is Reference Data, Not a Substitute for Engineering Analysis: These values inform underwriting risk grading and flag where closer attention to construction vulnerability is warranted — they are not a structural adequacy determination. Where a specific building's ability to meet current code demand is in genuine question (e.g. a pre-1970s unreinforced masonry building in a high Smax zone), the appropriate recommendation is a structural/seismic assessment by a qualified engineer, not a conclusion drawn from the climatic data alone.
